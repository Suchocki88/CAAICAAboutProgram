Understanding AI: A Conceptual and Experiential Approach
Teacher's Implementation Guide

Welcome to Understanding AI

This course represents a fundamentally different approach to teaching machine learning and artificial intelligence. Rather than targeting computer science or advanced math students, Understanding AI brings these critical concepts to social science students through hands-on experimentation, creative application, and critical analysis of AI's societal impact.

Your students won't write code or solve complex equations. Instead, they'll become AI researchers‚Äîconducting systematic experiments, documenting observations, and developing the literacy skills needed to navigate an AI-shaped world.

Course Philosophy

Why Social Sciences?

Social science students are uniquely positioned to understand AI's most important dimensions:

  • Ethical implications of algorithmic decision-making
  • Social impact of AI systems on communities and democracy
  • Bias and fairness in machine learning applications
  • Human behavior in relationship to AI technology
  • Policy questions surrounding AI regulation and governance

These students may not become AI engineers, but they'll become the citizens, policymakers, journalists, educators, counselors, and community leaders who shape how AI is developed, deployed, and regulated. They need AI literacy as urgently as any computer science major‚Äîperhaps more so.

The "Alia" Approach

Throughout this course, students work with Alia‚Äîthe name for our AI system customized to provide a directed and safe AI experience. Why give it a name?

  1. Demystification: A named entity feels less intimidating than "artificial intelligence system"
  2. Relationship building: Students develop a working partnership with their AI lab partner
  3. Consistency: The same system throughout ensures reliable progression of understanding
  4. Personal connection: Just as students might name a class pet, Alia becomes familiar

Students don't just chat with AI‚Äîthey conduct structured experiments, test hypotheses, and document findings scientifically. This transforms passive consumption into active investigation.

Course Structure Overview

0.5 Credit Semester Course

  • Duration: 16-17 weeks (approximately 88 lessons)
  • Meeting frequency: Daily periods (50 minutes recommended)
  • Credits: Half credit, typically satisfying social studies elective requirements

Four Lesson Types

The course alternates between four distinct lesson types, each serving specific pedagogical purposes:

1. Lesson Days (28 total)

Purpose: Introduce foundational concepts and terminology

Structure: Direct instruction, guided discussions, concept exploration

Student role: Active listening, note-taking, concept processing

2. Lab Days (25 total)

Purpose: Hands-on experimentation with Alia (AI system)

Structure: Structured experiments with hypothesis testing

Student role: Active researchers conducting experiments

Key component: Field journal documentation

3. Discussion Days (19 total)

Purpose: Analysis, reflection, synthesis, and peer learning

Structure: Socratic seminars, small group analysis, class debates

Student role: Critical thinkers, discussion participants

4. Project Days (8 total)

Purpose: Apply AI collaboration skills to creative writing

Structure: Independent work with AI as creative partner

Student role: Creative writers, AI collaborators

Assessment Days (8 total) provide formal evaluation of chapter understanding through written assessments, performance tasks, and portfolio reviews.

The Field Journal: Heart of Scientific Learning

What is the Field Journal?

The Field Journal is where students document every lab experiment throughout the semester. Think of it as a scientist's lab notebook‚Äîa systematic record of hypotheses, observations, analyses, and insights.

Why Field Journals Matter

For students:

  • Transforms scattered AI interactions into systematic investigation
  • Develops scientific thinking and documentation skills
  • Creates study resource for assessments
  • Builds habit of reflective practice
  • Demonstrates learning progression over time

For teachers:

  • Provides ongoing formative assessment data
  • Reveals student thinking and misconceptions
  • Documents student growth across semester
  • Offers alternative assessment for diverse learners
  • Creates accountability for lab engagement

Field Journal Structure

Each lab day includes a structured journal entry with scaffolded prompts. The scaffolding gradually releases over the semester:

Phase Chapters Scaffolding Level Student Focus
Early 1-3 Highly structured Complete hypothesis frameworks provided. Specific observation prompts. Guided analysis. Focus on accurate documentation.
Middle 4-6 Moderate scaffolding Hypothesis frameworks provided, students fill specifics. Observation categories given. Students connect to broader concepts.
Later 7-8 Minimal scaffolding Students generate own hypotheses. Self-directed observation and analysis. Independent connections to real-world applications.

Grading Field Journals

Field journals are NOT graded on "right answers"‚Äîthey're graded on:

Criterion Weight What to Look For
Completeness 40% All sections attempted. Evidence of effort and engagement. Timely completion.
Specificity 30% Concrete examples from experiments. Detailed observations (not vague generalizations). Accurate documentation of AI responses.
Thoughtfulness 30% Evidence of critical thinking. Connections between observations and concepts. Insightful questions and analysis.
Grading efficiency tip: Use a simple 3-point scale for each criterion: 3 = Exceeds expectations, 2 = Meets expectations, 1 = Needs improvement. Total: 9 possible points per entry. Quick calculation: (points earned / 9) √ó 100 = percentage grade.

The Creative Writing Project: Applying AI Collaboration

Project Overview

Throughout the semester, students develop a creative writing project (short story, narrative, or creative piece) in collaboration with Alia. This isn't "AI writes your story"‚Äîit's learning to use AI as a thinking partner while maintaining human creative control.

Project Days (8 total across semester)

  • Project Day 1 (Lesson 1.6): Story planning and AI brainstorming
  • Project Day 2 (Lesson 2.9): Character development with AI collaboration
  • Project Day 3 (Lesson 2.10): Pattern recognition in story structures
  • Project Day 4 (Lesson 3.9): Plot development using prompt engineering
  • Project Day 5 (Lesson 4.8): Using AI prediction for narrative choices
  • Project Day 6 (Lesson 5.7): Analyzing bias in creative AI suggestions
  • Project Day 7 (Lesson 7.7): Distinguishing authentic vs. AI content
  • Project Day 8 (Lesson 8.3 & 8.7): Final compilation and reflection

Why Creative Writing?

  1. Accessible to all students regardless of technical background
  2. Personally meaningful - students create something uniquely theirs
  3. Demonstrates AI collaboration skills in low-stakes environment
  4. Reveals AI capabilities and limitations through creative constraints
  5. Builds portfolio students can share beyond the course
  6. Engages different learning styles than traditional academic work
This is NOT an English class creative writing assignment. Students are assessed on their AI literacy skills, not their literary talent. Focus is on quality of AI collaboration, prompt engineering, and understanding of AI capabilities/limitations.

Grading Structure and Components

Course Grade Composition

The semester grade is calculated from five components:

Component Weight Description
Chapter Assignments 20% Questions embedded in lessons (8 chapters total)
Chapter Exams 40% End-of-chapter assessments (8 exams total)
Class Participation 10% Engagement during lesson and discussion days
Field Journal 15% Lab day documentation across all chapters
Creative Writing Project 15% Semester-long AI collaboration portfolio
Total 100%

Component 1: Chapter Assignments (20%)

What Are Chapter Assignments?

Chapter Assignments consist of questions embedded within the core lessons that students answer as they read or watch lesson content. These questions assess comprehension and engagement with the material.

The 4-Point Rubric System

Teachers grade each question using a simple 4-point scale:

  • 1 = Needs Work: Answer shows minimal understanding or effort
  • 2 = Making Progress: Answer shows partial understanding with gaps
  • 3 = You Got It: Answer demonstrates solid understanding (this is the target/perfect score)
  • 4 = Above and Beyond: Answer shows exceptional insight or connections beyond expectations
Important: A score of 3 ("You Got It") is considered perfect/100%. Students who earn a 3 on every question have fully mastered the material. A score of 4 is reserved for truly exceptional work that goes beyond what was asked.

Point Threshold for 100%

The point threshold for earning 100% on a Chapter Assignment = Number of Questions √ó 3

Example: If Chapter 2 has 15 questions:

  • Perfect score (100%) = 15 questions √ó 3 = 45 points
  • Student who earns all 3s = 45 points = 100%
  • Student who earns mix of 3s and 4s = more than 45 points = over 100% (extra credit)

Flexibility for Teachers

The platform allows teachers to customize their course by adding or removing questions from lessons as they see fit. Because of this flexibility, the exact number of questions per chapter may vary. Teachers should:

  1. Determine final question count for each chapter based on their customization
  2. Calculate point threshold: (Number of Questions √ó 3)
  3. Communicate this threshold to students at the start of each chapter
  4. Grade using the 1-4 rubric described above

There Are 8 Chapter Assignments

Since there are 8 chapters in the course, students complete 8 Chapter Assignments over the semester. Each Chapter Assignment counts equally toward the 20% total for this component.

Component 2: Chapter Exams (40%)

Chapter Exams are formal assessments given at the end of each chapter (8 exams total). These exams assess deeper understanding, application, and synthesis of chapter concepts. Format may include multiple choice, short answer, scenario analysis, and performance tasks.

Each exam counts equally toward the 40% total for this component.

Component 3: Class Participation (10%)

Class Participation includes:

  • Active engagement during Lesson Days and Discussion Days
  • Quality of contributions to class dialogue
  • Collaborative work during group activities
  • Professional conduct during Lab Days

Teachers should track participation throughout each chapter and assign grades at natural checkpoints (e.g., end of chapter, mid-semester, end of semester).

Component 4: Field Journal (15%)

Field Journal entries document all lab experiments throughout the semester. Students complete approximately 25-28 journal entries total across all lab days.

Grading Field Journal Entries:

Field journals are NOT graded on "right answers"‚Äîthey're graded on:

  • Completeness (40%): All sections attempted, evidence of effort
  • Specificity (30%): Concrete examples from experiments, detailed observations
  • Thoughtfulness (30%): Critical thinking, connections to concepts, insightful analysis

Suggested approach: Use a simple 3-point scale for each criterion (3 = Exceeds, 2 = Meets, 1 = Needs improvement). Total: 9 possible points per entry. Convert to percentage: (points earned / 9) √ó 100.

See the Field Journal section elsewhere in this guide for detailed grading information and scaffolding progression.

Component 5: Creative Writing Project (15%)

The Creative Writing Project is a semester-long portfolio developed through 8 Project Days across the course. Students are assessed on:

  • Quality of AI collaboration (not literary quality)
  • Evidence of thoughtful prompt engineering
  • Demonstration of understanding AI capabilities/limitations
  • Reflection on human-AI creative partnership
  • Creative risk-taking and experimentation

The project is assessed holistically at semester's end with a detailed rubric provided in the Creative Writing Project section of this guide.

Calculating Semester Grades

Final semester grade is calculated by combining the weighted percentages from all five components:

Grade Percentage Range Mastery Level
A 90-100% Exceptional AI literacy and engagement
B 80-89% Strong AI literacy and consistent effort
C 70-79% Adequate AI literacy and participation
D 60-69% Developing AI literacy, needs improvement
F Below 60% Insufficient progress

Why This Grading Structure?

  1. Balanced assessment: Multiple components measure different aspects of AI literacy
  2. Regular feedback: Students receive grades throughout semester, not just at end
  3. Flexibility: Teachers can customize assignments while maintaining consistent standards
  4. Growth-oriented: The 4-point rubric clarifies expectations and rewards solid understanding
  5. Multiple pathways: Students can demonstrate mastery through exams, journals, participation, and creative work

Differentiation and Support Strategies

For Students Who Need More Support

During Lesson Days:

  • Provide guided notes with sentence stems
  • Use visual organizers for complex concepts
  • Pre-teach vocabulary before lessons
  • Offer audio recordings of key content

During Lab Days:

  • Pair with confident partner
  • Provide simplified experiment protocols
  • Allow verbal response recording (transcribe later)
  • Break experiments into smaller steps
  • Extend time for journal documentation

For Field Journals:

  • Provide sentence stems and frames
  • Allow bullet points instead of paragraphs
  • Accept alternative documentation (voice memos, drawings with captions)
  • Conference individually to guide entries

For Advanced Students

During Lesson Days:

  • Provide extension readings/resources
  • Assign mini-research tasks on topics of interest
  • Invite to prepare brief presentations

During Lab Days:

  • Encourage more complex experiments
  • Challenge to design additional tests
  • Ask to document unexpected patterns
  • Provide advanced analysis questions

For Field Journals:

  • Encourage hypothesis generation beyond provided frameworks
  • Ask for connections to outside research/readings
  • Invite speculation about underlying mechanisms

English Language Learners

  • Provide vocabulary lists with definitions and visuals
  • Allow use of translation tools during AI experiments
  • Permit field journals in primary language (if teacher can assess)
  • Pair with bilingual buddies during group work
  • Use graphic organizers to structure thinking
  • Pre-teach idioms and culturally-specific references

Common Teacher Questions

"I'm not a computer science teacher. Can I really teach this?"

Yes, absolutely. This course is designed for social science teachers, not CS teachers. You don't need to understand how neural networks work at a technical level‚Äîyou need to facilitate student discovery of how AI behaves and impacts society. If you can moderate a discussion and guide student inquiry, you can teach this course.

The teacher's guides provide all technical background you need. You're learning alongside your students, which models lifelong learning perfectly.

"What if students ask technical questions I can't answer?"

This is an opportunity, not a problem. Respond with: "That's a great question. Let's design an experiment to investigate it." Or: "I don't know‚Äîlet's research that together."

You're teaching scientific thinking, not memorized facts. "I don't know, let's find out" is a perfectly appropriate teacher response in this course.

"How much time does grading field journals take?"

With practice, expect 15-20 minutes per set of 25 journals using the rubric approach. That's roughly 30-40 seconds per entry. You're not reading every word‚Äîyou're scanning for completion, specificity, and thoughtfulness.

Many teachers grade journals during students' independent project work time or as students complete chapter assessments.

"What if a student uses AI to write their field journal?"

This would be immediately obvious‚Äîfield journals document specific experiments each student conducted. AI can't accurately describe what Alia said to THIS student during THIS experiment.

If you suspect AI-generated journal entries, conference with the student: "Walk me through what happened when you asked Alia [specific question from their experiment]." Authentic entries will include specific details; AI-generated entries will be generic.

"What about students who don't have home internet access?"

All work can be completed during class time. Project days and lab days don't require homework. If students want to work outside class, provide time during school (study hall, library, before/after school) when possible.

"How do I handle inappropriate AI interactions?"

Set clear expectations in Week 1: "Alia is our class learning tool, like a textbook. Treat it professionally."

Claude.ai has built-in safety features and won't engage with inappropriate content. If students try to misuse it, they'll simply get a refusal response‚Äîwhich itself becomes a learning moment about AI safety guardrails.

"What if my school blocks Claude.ai?"

Work with your IT department before the course starts to whitelist claude.ai. Explain this is an educational AI literacy course, not recreational AI use.

Pacing and Flexibility

Recommended Weekly Pattern

Sample weekly schedule for daily 50-minute periods:

  • Monday: Lesson Day (new content introduction)
  • Tuesday: Lab Day (hands-on experimentation)
  • Wednesday: Discussion Day (analysis and reflection)
  • Thursday: Lesson Day or Lab Day (depending on chapter)
  • Friday: Project Day, Discussion Day, or Assessment (varies by chapter)

Chapter Length Variability

Chapters vary in length (7-13 lessons) based on content complexity:

  • Shorter chapters (1, 7): Foundational or application-focused
  • Longer chapters (2, 3, 6): Conceptually dense with extended practice

"Flex Days"

Some chapters include explicit flex days for:

  • Catching up struggling students
  • Deepening learning for advanced students
  • Addressing unexpected school closures
  • Revisiting concepts before assessments
  • Additional project work time
If you lose instructional time: Prioritize lab days (hands-on learning is irreplaceable). Combine related discussion days. Assign lesson day content as independent reading. Extend chapter slightly rather than rushing.
